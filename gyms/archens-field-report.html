Trail 1
The Observation Lab
"Before a field scientist writes anything, they look. Really look. This trail is about noticing details most people walk right past."
Your Mission:
1
Pick one fossil you've worked on at Burpee — or one you remember clearly. (It can be a photo on your phone, a memory, or something you're currently cleaning.)
2
Spend 2 minutes just looking at it. Set a timer. Don't write yet — just observe. Notice texture, color, shape, size, weight, anything unusual.
3
Now record 5 specific observations. Not "it's brown" — more like "reddish-brown with small white mineral deposits near the left edge." Real paleontologists call these field notes.
4
Add one question your observations made you wonder about. ("Why does this section look different from the rest?" counts perfectly.)
Show Your Work As:
📝 Written notes 🎙️ Voice memo ✏️ Labeled sketch 📸 Annotated photo
📜
Trail 2
The Claim + Evidence Move
"Here's the one writing move that makes scientists sound like scientists: you make a CLAIM, then you back it up with EVIDENCE. It's the same thing you do when you tell someone about a cool fossil — you don't just say 'it was cool,' you say WHY."
Your Mission:
1
Look at your Trail 1 observations. Find the most interesting one. That's your Evidence.
2
Now write a Claim sentence — one sentence that tells us something you believe to be TRUE about this fossil based on what you saw. Example: "This fossil likely came from a young animal because the bone structure appears less dense than adult specimens I've seen."
3
Write a Reasoning sentence that connects your claim to your evidence. Use a connector phrase: "This suggests…" or "This is consistent with…" or "One explanation might be…"
4
You now have a 3-sentence field note: Observation → Claim → Reasoning. That's the core of every scientific report ever written.
Show Your Work As:
📝 3 written sentences 🎙️ Spoken aloud to parent 💻 Typed document 🎬 Short video log
⭐
Main Challenge
The Burpee Field Report
"Real paleontologists write a Field Report after every session in the lab or at a dig site. It's not a school essay — it's a scientific record. Yours will go into your Fossil Lab portfolio. Future Cole will thank current Cole for this."
Your Mission:
1
Choose a Burpee visit — it can be recent or one you remember well. You're writing a Field Report for that session.
2
Your Field Report needs these 4 sections (they can be as short as 2–3 sentences each):

📍 Location & Date — where, when, what your role was
🔬 What I Worked On — what fossil, what tools, what techniques
🧠 What I Observed — your Trail 1 notes, expanded
💡 What I'm Wondering — your question, plus any hypothesis
3
Add one Claim + Evidence sentence from Trail 2 somewhere in the report. This upgrades it from a log to a scientific document.
4
Give your report a title the way real paleontologists do: "Observations on Specimen BM-2024-14: Possible Ceratopsid Frill Fragment" — or whatever fits your fossil.

📄 Written or typed report 🎙️ Audio report read aloud 🖼️ Illustrated field log 📹 Video field log

📋 Parent Guide — Read Before Starting

What this gym is really doing: It's building the one writing skill that matters most for high school — making a claim and supporting it with evidence. But because it's anchored in Cole's real Burpee work, it never feels like a school essay. He's already doing the science. We're just teaching him to write it down the way scientists do.

Trail 1 tip: The 2-minute "just observe" pause is important. It builds the habit of slowing down before writing — something most students (and most writers) skip. If Cole wants to start writing before the timer ends, gently remind him that even the best paleontologists take notes in pencil first.

Trail 2 tip: The hardest part is the Claim sentence — it requires him to commit to a statement. If he says "I don't know," redirect: "What's your best guess based on what you actually saw?" A hypothesis doesn't have to be correct to be scientific.

Main Challenge tip: This Field Report goes straight into his Fossil Lab portfolio. Frame it that way from the start — "This is your official record of that session." That shifts it from schoolwork to real work. You can use the same format every Burpee visit going forward — it becomes a running scientific log.

Time estimate: Trail 1 = 15–20 min. Trail 2 = 15 min. Main Challenge = 20–30 min. All three in one session is ambitious — two sessions is ideal.
