Catcher Gym

🚨 Urgent Quest Alert
Gengar is on the loose — and he's stealing stories.

Books, movies, comics — he's been slipping into them at night and hiding their best parts in the shadows. Trainers everywhere are trying to read and nothing makes sense. They can't figure out WHY Gengar keeps taking them — or what he plans to do with all those stolen laughs and plot twists.

The only way to catch him? Figure out what makes each story tick. Once you understand how a story works — how it makes you laugh, how it pulls you in — Gengar loses his grip. The stories come back. The trainers can read again.

Three stories. Three traps. One very annoyed Gengar. 👻

Trial 1 · Written Story
The Written Trap
▼
Gengar's first hiding spot: an old story where two bumbling adults try to kidnap a kid — and the kid is SO chaotic that they end up paying the dad to take him back. Gengar loves this one. The little gremlin kid reminds him of himself.
🔗
The Ransom of Red Chief by O. Henry — free, full story
americanliterature.com → O. Henry → The Ransom of Red Chief
  • Read the story — or have it read aloud. It's pretty short. The kid's name is Red Chief and he is absolutely feral. You'll see.
  • Notice when you laugh (or want to laugh). What just happened right before that moment?
  • Pick one moment that was the funniest or the most surprising. Could be a line, could be what a character did. Just one.
  • Tell someone (or write/type/speak into a voice memo): "The funniest part was ___ because ___." That's it. One sentence is enough to trap Gengar here. YOUR CHOICE: speak · type · write
Why this story: It's a classic comedy of reversal — the "powerful" adults end up helpless, and the chaotic kid wins. Cole's pizza-ordering humor instinct is exactly this: unexpected absurdism that derails the "serious" plan. He'll recognize the logic immediately.

Time estimate: 20–35 minutes total. The story is about 4,000 words — manageable in one sit or split across two short sessions.

Reading options: Read together, he reads independently, or use the VOA Learning English simplified version (also verified free at learningenglish.voanews.com — search "Ransom of Red Chief"). The simplified version is shorter and easier if needed.

The output goal: One sentence. "The funniest part was ___ because ___." That's the ELA skill — noticing what creates the effect and naming it. Don't push for more than that on Trial 1.

Wisconsin standards: RL.8.3 (character interaction), RL.8.6 (point of view/purpose)

Trial 2 · Comic Strip
The Visual Trap
🔒
Gengar's second hiding spot: a comic strip. No paragraphs. No chapters. Just panels, pictures, and a punchline face in the last box. Gengar thinks you can't figure out how a story works if there are no words — but he's wrong.
🔗
Garfield by Jim Davis — free daily strips
gocomics.com/garfield
Browse the archive and pick any strip where Garfield's plan backfires — look for the face in the last panel. That face IS the punchline.
  • Browse a few Garfield strips until one makes you want to laugh (or groan). Pick that one.
  • Look at it panel by panel. What happens in Panel 1? What changes in Panel 2? What's the payoff in Panel 3?
  • Notice: how does the artist show a feeling without writing it? What does Garfield's face DO in the last panel?
  • Compare to Trial 1: the written story used words to describe a funny face. The comic just SHOWED the face. Which hit harder — reading it or seeing it? YOUR CHOICE: say it · write it · draw a comparison
Why Garfield: The entire strip usually has one "turn" — setup → small escalation → face. It's the purest possible example of how visual storytelling compresses narrative. Cole already loves Yzma's expression-based humor; Garfield works the same way in 3 panels.

Time estimate: 15–20 minutes. Browsing is part of the task — that's reading comprehension in disguise.

The ELA skill being built: RL.8.7 — comparing how different media convey a story. Cole doesn't need to name that standard. He just needs to notice that the comic "showed" what the written story "told."

Good strip types to look for: Any strip where Garfield attempts something (steal food, avoid Monday, outwit Odie) and it backfires — those have the clearest setup → reversal → expression arc. The Monday strips are a reliable go-to.

Wisconsin standards: RL.8.7 (compare/contrast across media formats), RL.8.6 (author's craft and purpose)

Main Challenge · Movie
The Final Trap
🔒
Gengar saved the best story for last — the one with the villain who can't catch a break, the sidekick who suffers, and the waterfall that ruins everything. He thinks a movie is too big to figure out. He's wrong about that too.
🎬
The Emperor's New Groove (Disney, 2000)
Start with the waterfall clip you already know:
youtube.com/watch?v=fhOrxkGlLDM
Then watch the full movie — or as much as you want. Pay special attention to Yzma's faces when her plans fall apart.
  • Watch the waterfall clip first. Notice: who has the power at the start? Who ends up winning? How did that flip happen?
  • Watch Yzma's face in at least 3 moments where her plans go wrong. What does her face do? How does the animator SHOW frustration without saying it in words?
  • Think back across all three trials. Written story → Comic strip → Movie. Each one made something funny. Did they all use the same trick? Or different tricks?
  • Write, type, or speak your paragraph — 3 to 5 sentences. Gengar's question is:

    "How did they get you to care AND laugh at the same time?"

    Not a summary. Not a review. Just: what was the trick? Pick any of the three stories (or all of them). YOUR CHOICE: type · speak → transcribe · handwrite
The writing goal: 3–5 sentences is the stamina target. The question ("how did they get you to care AND laugh?") is designed to pull a response out rather than feel like an assignment. It's asking Cole to be the expert — because he is.

What counts as a good response: Any 3–5 sentences that name a specific moment and say something about why it worked. "Kuzco was annoying at the start so when he fell off the waterfall I laughed instead of feeling bad" is a GREAT answer. It has observation + reasoning.

Output scaffolding:
  • Speech-to-text: Cole speaks, you or an app transcribes, he edits if he wants
  • Voice memo: just record it — that counts as the output
  • Typed: any format, any device
  • Handwritten: perfectly fine, transcript note for the record
Do NOT: Ask for a rewrite unless Cole wants to. First draft IS the draft.

Connecting the three trials: The underlying skill built across this Gym is RL.8.7 — analyzing how different media use different storytelling tools. Written story used exaggerated language and irony. Comic used visual expression and timing. Movie used animation, sound, and character design. Same goal (get you to laugh) — different tools.

Wisconsin standards: RL.8.1 (cite evidence), RL.8.3 (analyze character), RL.8.7 (compare media), W.8.4 (clear writing), SL.8.4 (if spoken)
