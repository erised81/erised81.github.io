Trail 1
The Source Scan
"Rotom-Dex has seen a lot of Trainers trust bad information. Before you use a source, you run it through the Scanner. Let's see what yours actually detects."
Your Mission:
1
Think of a paleontology topic you actually want to know more about. (It can be a dinosaur, a dig site, an extinction theory — anything real.)
2
Find three sources about it. Try to get one from each category: a museum or university website, a YouTube video, and a wiki or fan site.
3
Run each source through Rotom's 3-Question Scanner:

🔹 Who made this? (A scientist? A student? Unknown?)
🔹 How do they know? (Do they cite other sources? Show evidence?)
🔹 Could they be wrong? (Is this opinion, theory, or confirmed fact?)
4
Rate each source: Strong / OK / Weak — and write one sentence explaining why.
✅ Strong Source Expert author, cites research, peer-reviewed or museum-affiliated
⚠️ OK Source Seems accurate but unclear who wrote it or where info came from
❌ Weak Source No author, no citations, lots of opinions presented as facts
💡 Pro Move Even a "weak" source can point you toward a strong one — check what they link to
Show Your Work As:
📋 Written source log 🎙️ Talked through aloud 📊 Simple 3-column table
📑
Trail 2
The Note-Taking Technique
"Even the best Trainer forgets things. Rotom-Dex records everything so nothing is lost. Your brain is the same — taking notes during research isn't about writing everything down. It's about capturing the parts that MATTER."
Your Mission:
1
Pick your strongest source from Trail 1. Read or watch it all the way through once — just to understand it. Don't take notes yet.
2
Now go through it again. Look for exactly 3 things to capture:

📌 One surprising or new fact
📌 One thing you already knew (but now have a source for)
📌 One thing you're now questioning or want to dig deeper on
3
For each note: write it in your own words. Don't copy the exact sentence. This is what scientists call paraphrasing — it proves you actually understand it, not just that you can copy it.
4
At the bottom, write the source name (website name or video title). You just created a cited research note. That's a real academic skill.
Show Your Work As:
📝 3 written notes + source 🎙️ Voice note per item 🗂️ Index card notes 💻 Digital research doc
⭐
Main Challenge
The Synthesis Scan
"Rotom-Dex doesn't just store data — it CONNECTS data. The most powerful research skill isn't finding information. It's finding out when two sources agree, when they disagree, and what that tells you. That's synthesis. That's what separates scientists from searchers."
Your Mission:
1
Using the sources and notes from Trails 1 and 2, find one thing two of your sources agree on. Write a sentence: "Both [source A] and [source B] say that…"
2
Find one place where your sources say something different — or where one source has information the other doesn't mention. Write: "[Source A] says X, but [Source B] says Y — this might mean…"
3
Write a Summary Statement — 2 to 3 sentences that answer: "What do I now know about [your topic] that I didn't know before?" Use at least one piece of evidence from your notes.
4
Bonus Rotom Spark: Write one follow-up question — something your research made you want to investigate next. Real researchers always end with the next question. That's how science keeps moving.

📋 Parent Guide — Read Before Starting

What this gym is really doing: Research skills are the single biggest predictor of high school academic success. Cole already researches naturally — he watches PBS Eons, looks things up, uses the Burpee library. This gym teaches him the formal version of what he already does instinctively, which makes it feel like leveling up rather than starting from scratch.

Trail 1 tip: The Source Scanner is designed to be quick — don't over-complicate it. The goal is just to build the habit of pausing before trusting a source. A Wikipedia article can be rated "OK" (not bad, just needs verification) — that's a nuanced, accurate takeaway.

Trail 2 tip: The "read once without notes, then go back" technique is real study skill research. It improves comprehension significantly. If Cole resists doing it twice, let him try taking notes on first read — then ask if he wishes he'd waited. One experience is worth more than explaining it.

Main Challenge tip: The "synthesis" step (comparing sources) is genuinely a high-school-level skill. It's okay if it feels hard. The sentence starters are scaffolds — let him lean on them fully. Over time they become internalized. The goal is the thinking, not the sentence structure.

Topic suggestions if Cole needs a starting point: Carcharodontosaurus vs. T. rex size debate · What actually killed off non-avian dinosaurs · How we know what color dinosaurs were · Soft tissue discoveries in fossils — all of these have genuinely conflicting sources, which makes Trail 2 and the Main Challenge more interesting.
