Trail 1
Scale & Proportion
"Every fossil drawing in a science journal is drawn to scale — meaning the sizes are all proportional to each other, even if the paper is tiny. This is the math that lets you compare a T. rex skull to a Microraptor in the same diagram."
Your Mission:
1
Look up (or use what you know) the length of three dinosaurs — one small, one medium, one large. For example: Microraptor (~1m), Iguanodon (~9m), Patagotitan (~37m).
2
Choose a scale to draw them on paper. A common one: 1 centimeter = 1 meter of real dinosaur. That means Microraptor = 1cm, Iguanodon = 9cm, Patagotitan = 37cm.
3
Draw a simple scale silhouette of each one — just the outline is fine. Label the real size and the scale size next to each.
4
Answer the Scale Question: If you wanted to fit all three on a single sheet of A4 paper, what scale would work? (Hint: how wide is an A4 sheet in cm?)
🔢 The Math Hiding Here:
This is ratios and proportional reasoning — the same math used in architecture, mapmaking, and video game design. When Cole builds his Brachiosaurus game in Scratch, he's already thinking about scale every time he sizes a sprite.
Real length ÷ Scale factor = Drawing length
37m ÷ 1 = 37cm on paper
37m ÷ 2 = 18.5cm on paper (1cm = 2m scale)

Trail 2
Deep Time Numbers
"Paleontology deals with numbers so large that regular notation breaks down. 65 million years. 252 million years. 540 million years. Scientists write these using a shortcut called scientific notation — and once you see it, you'll notice it everywhere."
Your Mission:
1
Look at this timeline — fill in the missing scientific notation (or verify the ones shown):

65,000,000 years ago = 6.5 × 10⁷
252,000,000 years ago = ?
540,000,000 years ago = ?
4,500,000,000 years (Earth's age) = ?
2
Estimation challenge: If non-avian dinosaurs existed for about 165 million years, and modern humans have existed for about 300,000 years — how many times longer did dinosaurs exist than humans? Show your calculation.
3
Find a fossil from the Burpee collection (from a visit or memory). Write down approximately how old it is. Convert that number to scientific notation. You just described your own fossil mathematically.
🔢 The Math Hiding Here:
Scientific notation is an 8th grade math standard — and it exists specifically because scientists needed a way to write enormous (and tiny) numbers cleanly. Paleontology is one of the most natural real-world homes for it.
165,000,000 ÷ 300,000 = 550
Dinosaurs existed ~550× longer than modern humans

252,000,000 = 2.52 × 10⁸

Main Challenge
The Brachiosaurus Biomechanics Problem
"Here's one of the great open questions in paleontology: How did Brachiosaurus eat enough? A 60-ton animal needs enormous amounts of food each day. Let's use math to find out if it's even possible — using the same data real scientists work with."
Your Mission:
1
Start with the known data:

Estimated mass: 30,000–60,000 kg
Estimated daily food need: ~400 kg of plant material
Estimated neck reach: up to 9 meters high

These are real estimates from fossil studies. Write them down.
2
The Food Math: If Brachiosaurus needed 400kg of leaves per day, and each tree in the Jurassic had roughly 50kg of edible leaves available per visit — how many trees would it need to strip per day? Show the division.
3
The Neck Math: If its neck could reach 9m high and it took 3 steps between feeding spots, estimate how many feeding spots it could reach in a 1km path. (You can estimate or draw this out.)
4
Write a Conclusion: Based on your math, do you think Brachiosaurus could realistically meet its food needs? Write 2–3 sentences using your numbers as evidence. This is what a scientist's conclusion looks like.
5
Bonus Deep Time Badge: Apply your Trail 1 scale to draw Brachiosaurus next to your house (or a known landmark). How tall is your house compared to its shoulder height of ~6 meters?

📋 Parent Guide — Read Before Starting

What this gym is really doing: This gym brings math to Cole through the door he already has wide open — paleontology. Scale/proportion (Trail 1) and scientific notation (Trail 2) are both 6th–8th grade math standards. The Main Challenge applies them together in a genuine problem-solving context. None of this is worksheet math — it's applied math with real stakes he cares about.

Trail 1 tip: The A4 paper question at the end of Trail 1 is open-ended — there's no single right answer, and that's intentional. What matters is that he works through the logic. If he gets stuck, offer: "How wide is the paper? What's the biggest dinosaur? If that one has to fit, what's the biggest scale you could use?"

Trail 2 tip: Scientific notation can feel abstract. Anchor it: "It's just a shortcut so scientists don't have to write all those zeros." The estimation problem (550× longer than humans) is usually surprising and tends to spark conversation. Let that go wherever it goes — that's the real learning.

Main Challenge tip: The numbers given are reasonable estimates — not exact. That's fine, and worth saying upfront: "Scientists work with estimates all the time when they're studying animals that lived 150 million years ago. Your math doesn't need to be perfect to be science." This is a growth mindset anchor for math avoidance.

Tutor connection: These problems can be shared with Cole's math tutor as a preview or follow-up. They directly connect to the ratio, proportion, and scientific notation standards the tutor will be working on — same skills, different wrapper.
